A DJ Data Forward
Open in new tab
Why This Option
Puts BPM and Camelot key in the leftmost data columns, making them the first thing the eye hits after album art. This is "mix data first" — optimized for the moment a DJ is scanning for compatible tracks to follow the current one. Column order: Art → BPM → Key → Title → Artist → Genre → Source.
Works Well For
Active set building where you already know roughly what you want and you're scanning for BPM/key matches. Power users who've internalized their collection and just need the numbers.
Watch Out For
Pushes Artist to tertiary emphasis (12px muted) — too low if you think of your collection in terms of "who." Doesn't include energy level. Imposes a fixed scanning order that may not match all DJ tasks.
B Identity First
Open in new tab
Why This Option
Traditional music library order: Title → Artist → BPM → Key → Genre → Source. The track's identity answers "what is this?" before "can I mix it?" BPM and Key differentiate through visual treatment (monospace, color chips) rather than positional dominance. Familiar if you've used any media library.
Works Well For
Browsing and discovery — when you're exploring your collection, not actively building a set. Users transitioning from consumer music apps. The conventional order is instantly learnable.
Watch Out For
Elevates Title over Artist — DJs often think "that Fisher track" not "that Losing It track." BPM/Key don't get enough structural emphasis for quick scanning when you're deep in set building. Missing energy level. Could be any media library — loses DJ-specific character.
C Flat Five
Open in new tab
Why This Option
Five core fields at co-primary emphasis: Artist, Title, BPM, Key, Energy. No single column dominates — each is differentiated by visual treatment: bold text (artist), regular weight (title), monospace number (BPM), color chip (key), bar graph (energy). Column order: Art → Artist → Title → BPM → Key → Energy → Genre → Source. Artist leads because DJs think "who" before "what."
Works Well For
All DJ tasks — browsing (eye hits artist/title) and set building (eye jumps to BPM/key/energy). The flat hierarchy lets the DJ's current task determine the scan pattern, not the layout. Mirrors how Rekordbox and Traktor present column data. Includes energy level for shaping the set arc.
Watch Out For
Flat hierarchy = less visual drama. No obvious "hero" element in each row, which could feel undifferentiated to new users. More visual coding complexity for the color system (energy bar colors + Camelot chip colors). Only 3 emphasis levels — less room for the typography facet to create layered hierarchy.
My Take
Go with C (Flat Five)

You told me DJs think in terms of artist, title, BPM, key, and energy — all five matter. Options A and B both try to pick a winner between identity and DJ data, but the real answer is neither dominates. The DJ's current task determines what they scan for.

A flat hierarchy with rich visual coding is exactly how Rekordbox and Traktor work — every column is equally accessible, and you scan to whatever matters right now. The visual coding approach (bold vs. regular vs. mono vs. chip vs. bars) gives each field its own "shape" so the eye learns to jump directly to what it needs.

Option A's approach of making BPM/Key physically larger is heavy-handed — it pre-decides the DJ's task. Option B's traditional library ordering demotes the mix data that makes this product special.

The energy column is the kicker. Energy is core to set building — you need to shape the night's arc, not just match BPM and key. A and B both miss it entirely.

Trade-off: a flat hierarchy gives the typography and color facets less room to create dramatic visual layers. But for a dense, scannable catalog, that's the right trade. This is a power-user tool.
